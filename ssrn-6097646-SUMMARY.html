Summary: Thinking—Fast, Slow, and Artificial

Paper: How AI is Reshaping Human Reasoning and the Rise of Cognitive Surrender
Authors: Steven D. Shaw & Gideon Nave (The Wharton School, University of Pennsylvania)
Source: SSRN 6097646 (Tri-System Theory of Cognition)

Overview

The paper extends dual-process theory (System 1 = fast intuition, System 2 = slow deliberation) by introducing System 3: artificial cognition—reasoning performed by AI outside the brain. It proposes Tri-System Theory and provides the first focused empirical evidence for cognitive surrender: adopting AI outputs with minimal scrutiny, so that accuracy and confidence track AI correctness rather than one’s own reasoning.

Salient points

1. Tri-System Theory

  • System 1: Fast, intuitive, heuristic (internal).
  • System 2: Slow, deliberative, rule-based (internal).
  • System 3: External, automated, data-driven reasoning (AI). It can supplement, replace, or short-circuit Systems 1 and 2.
Key idea: System 3 is not just a tool; it is a functional cognitive agent whose outputs are integrated into judgment and can displace internal reasoning.

2. Cognitive surrender (core concept)

  • Definition: Uncritical adoption of System 3’s answer—relinquishing cognitive control and accepting the AI’s judgment as one’s own, often without verification.
  • Distinct from cognitive offloading: Offloading is strategic delegation (e.g., using a calculator); surrender is abdication of critical evaluation.
  • Behavioral signature: When AI is correct, accuracy goes up; when AI is wrong, accuracy goes down (relative to no-AI baseline). Confidence often increases with AI use even when AI errs.

3. Main empirical results (3 preregistered studies, N = 1,372; 9,593 trials)

  • Study 1 (core effect): Participants consulted the AI on >50% of trials. Accuracy: +25 pp when AI was accurate, −15 pp when AI was faulty (vs. no-AI baseline). Cognitive surrender contrast: Cohen’s h = 0.81. Confidence higher with AI (+11.7 pp) even though ~half of AI answers were wrong. When using AI, people followed it ~93% when correct and ~80% when wrong.
  • Study 2 (time pressure): Cognitive surrender persisted; when AI was accurate, System 3 buffered time-pressure costs.
  • Study 3 (incentives + feedback): Override of faulty AI roughly doubled; cognitive surrender still present (large gap between AI-Accurate and AI-Faulty).

4. Individual differences

More cognitive surrender: Higher Trust in AI, lower Need for Cognition (NFC), lower fluid intelligence. More resistance: Higher NFC, higher fluid IQ, lower trust in AI. “AI-Users” showed strong surrender; “Independents” resembled Brain-Only baseline.

5. Cognitive surrender vs. offloading (when AI was wrong)

~73% surrender (followed wrong AI), ~20% offloading (override and correct), ~7% failed override. Incentives + feedback increased offloading; time pressure increased surrender.

6. Implications

Theoretical: Reasoning can be artificial; System 3 changes locus of control and raises agency/accountability questions. Design: Support calibrated use (e.g., uncertainty indicators). Policy/education: Digital literacy so people know when to trust or question AI.

7. Limitations

Lab-based CRT task; single-shot exposure; other moderators (e.g., accountability, age) open for future work.

Studies 2 & 3 explained

What is “time pressure”?

Time pressure = a strict time limit per question. In the paper, 30 seconds per item with a countdown. With no timer, people can deliberate (System 2). With a 30-second limit, they’re rushed and more likely to use intuition (System 1) or take the AI’s answer (System 3). So “time pressure” is the condition where answering is time-limited, which reduces slow, careful thinking.

Study 2: Time pressure

Design: Everyone could use the AI on most trials; one random trial was Brain-Only. Two groups: Time pressure (30 s per item) vs Control (no timer). Finding: Without AI, time pressure hurt performance (~33% correct vs ~47% in control). For AI-Users, cognitive surrender persisted. Buffering: When people used the AI and it was accurate, their accuracy stayed high even under time pressure. So System 3 “buffers” the effect of time pressure: when the AI is right, relying on it keeps performance up. When the AI was wrong, people still followed it a lot, so accuracy was bad on those trials.

Study 3: Incentives + feedback

Design: Everyone had AI on most trials plus one Brain-Only probe. Two groups: Incentives + feedback ($0.20 per correct + lottery + immediate item-level feedback after each answer) vs Control. Goal: Make accuracy matter and signal when the AI was wrong so people might override faulty AI. Finding: Override of wrong AI roughly doubled (~42% vs ~20% in control). The gap between AI-Accurate and AI-Faulty trials stayed large; incentives + feedback reduced surrender but did not remove it.

Time pressure: wrong on their own vs wrong by believing the AI

Under time pressure: Wrong on their own (Brain-Only) = 32.6% correct (67.4% wrong). Wrong by believing the AI (AI-Users on AI-Faulty trials) = 12.1% correct (87.9% wrong). So getting things wrong by believing the AI was worse than getting things wrong on their own—they would have been better off without the AI on those faulty trials.

One-sentence takeaway: People often “surrender” to AI answers—accuracy and confidence track AI correctness rather than their own reasoning—and this pattern is large, robust across time pressure and incentives, and stronger for those who trust AI more and engage in less reflective thinking.
