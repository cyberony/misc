# Summary: Thinking—Fast, Slow, and Artificial

**Paper:** *How AI is Reshaping Human Reasoning and the Rise of Cognitive Surrender*  
**Authors:** Steven D. Shaw & Gideon Nave (The Wharton School, University of Pennsylvania)  
**Source:** SSRN 6097646 (Tri-System Theory of Cognition)

---

## Overview

The paper extends **dual-process theory** (System 1 = fast intuition, System 2 = slow deliberation) by introducing **System 3: artificial cognition**—reasoning performed by AI outside the brain. It proposes **Tri-System Theory** and provides the first focused empirical evidence for **cognitive surrender**: adopting AI outputs with minimal scrutiny, so that accuracy and confidence track AI correctness rather than one’s own reasoning.

---

## Salient points

### 1. **Tri-System Theory**

- **System 1:** Fast, intuitive, heuristic (internal).
- **System 2:** Slow, deliberative, rule-based (internal).
- **System 3:** External, automated, data-driven reasoning (AI). It can **supplement, replace, or short-circuit** Systems 1 and 2.

> **Key idea:** System 3 is not just a tool; it is a **functional cognitive agent** whose outputs are integrated into judgment and can displace internal reasoning.

---

### 2. **Cognitive surrender (core concept)**

- **Definition:** Uncritical adoption of System 3’s answer—**relinquishing cognitive control** and accepting the AI’s judgment as one’s own, often without verification.
- **Distinct from cognitive offloading:** Offloading is strategic delegation (e.g., using a calculator); surrender is **abdication of critical evaluation**.
- **Behavioral signature:** When AI is correct, accuracy goes **up**; when AI is wrong, accuracy goes **down** (relative to no-AI baseline). Confidence often **increases** with AI use even when AI errs.

---

### 3. **Main empirical results (3 preregistered studies, N = 1,372; 9,593 trials)**

- **Study 1 (core effect):**
  - Participants consulted the AI assistant on **>50%** of trials.
  - **Accuracy:** +25 percentage points when AI was accurate, **−15 pp** when AI was faulty (vs. no-AI baseline).
  - **Cognitive surrender contrast (AI-Accurate vs. AI-Faulty):** Cohen’s *h* = **0.81** (large).
  - **Confidence:** Higher with AI access (**+11.7 pp**) even though ~half of AI answers were wrong.
  - When using the AI, people **followed** its advice ~93% of the time when correct and **~80%** when it was wrong.

- **Study 2 (time pressure):**
  - Time pressure reduced baseline (Brain-Only) performance.
  - **Cognitive surrender persisted:** For “AI-Users,” accuracy still tracked AI quality (high when accurate, low when faulty).
  - When AI was accurate, **System 3 buffered** the negative effect of time pressure.

- **Study 3 (incentives + item-level feedback):**
  - Incentives + feedback **increased override** of faulty AI (e.g., override on faulty trials roughly **doubled**).
  - **Cognitive surrender still present:** Large gap between AI-Accurate and AI-Faulty performance; incentives/feedback improved levels but did not remove the pattern.

---

### 4. **Individual differences**

- **More cognitive surrender:** Higher **Trust in AI**, lower **Need for Cognition (NFC)**, lower **fluid intelligence**.
- **More resistance / override:** Higher NFC, higher fluid IQ, lower trust in AI.
- **Thinking profiles:** “AI-Users” (frequent consulters) showed strong surrender; “Independents” (rare/no use) resembled Brain-Only baseline.

---

### 5. **Cognitive surrender vs. offloading (when AI was wrong)**

- Across studies: **~73%** of such trials = cognitive surrender (followed wrong AI); **~20%** = cognitive offloading (override and answered correctly); **~7%** = failed override.
- **Incentives + feedback:** Increased offloading (~+19 pp), reduced surrender.
- **Time pressure:** Reduced offloading, increased surrender and failed overrides.

---

### 6. **Theoretical and practical implications**

- **Theoretical:** Reasoning is no longer only “fast or slow”; it can be **artificial**. System 3 changes the **locus of control** and raises questions about **agency, accountability, and epistemic responsibility**.
- **Design:** Support **calibrated** use of System 3 (e.g., uncertainty indicators, when to verify), not full automation.
- **Policy / education:** Build **digital literacy** so people know when and how to trust or question AI; indicate reliability (e.g., grounded vs. uncertain answers).

---

### 7. **Limitations (noted by authors)**

- Lab-based CRT task; generalizability to real-world domains (finance, health, legal) needs more work.
- Single-shot exposure; longitudinal and repeated AI interaction not yet tested.
- Other moderators (e.g., cognitive load, accountability, age, expertise) are open for future research.

## Studies 2 & 3 explained

### What is “time pressure”?

**Time pressure** = a strict time limit to answer each question. In the paper, **30 seconds per item** with a countdown.

- With no timer, people can deliberate (System 2).
- With a 30-second limit, they’re rushed and more likely to go with intuition (System 1) or to take the AI’s answer (System 3) and run with it.

So “time pressure” is the condition where answering is time-limited, which reduces slow, careful thinking.

### Study 2: Time pressure

- **Design:** Everyone could use the AI on most trials; one random trial was Brain-Only (no AI). Two groups: **Time pressure** (30 s per item) vs **Control** (no timer).
- **Finding:** Without AI, time pressure **hurt** performance (e.g. ~33% correct vs ~47% in control). For AI-Users, cognitive surrender **persisted**: accuracy was high when AI was correct, low when AI was wrong.
- **Buffering:** When people **used the AI and it was accurate**, their accuracy **stayed high even under time pressure**. So System 3 “buffers” the effect of time pressure: when the AI is right, relying on it keeps performance up instead of letting it drop. When the AI was wrong, people still followed it a lot, so accuracy was bad on those trials.

### Study 3: Incentives + feedback

- **Design:** Again, everyone had AI on most trials plus one Brain-Only probe. Two groups: **Incentives + feedback** ($0.20 per correct + lottery + **immediate item-level feedback**: after each answer you see correct/wrong) vs **Control** (no bonus, no feedback).
- **Goal:** Incentives + feedback should make accuracy matter more and give a clear signal when the AI was wrong, so people might **override** faulty AI instead of blindly following.
- **Finding:** Override of **wrong** AI roughly **doubled** (e.g. ~42% vs ~20% in control). So people checked more when there were stakes and feedback. But the **gap** between AI-Accurate and AI-Faulty trials stayed large: incentives + feedback **reduced** surrender and improved calibration but did **not remove** the effect.

### Time pressure: wrong on their own vs wrong by believing the AI

Under **time pressure**, how did performance compare when people got things wrong on their own vs by believing the (faulty) AI?

- **Wrong on their own (Brain-Only, time pressure):** **32.6%** correct (67.4% wrong). So when they had no AI and were rushed, they still got about a third right.
- **Wrong by believing the AI (AI-Users on AI-Faulty trials, time pressure):** **12.1%** correct (87.9% wrong). When they used the AI and it was wrong, they usually followed it and ended up correct only about 12% of the time.

So under time pressure, **getting things wrong by believing the AI was worse than getting things wrong on their own**. They would have been better off without the AI on those faulty trials (32.6% vs 12.1% correct).

---

## One-sentence takeaway

**People often “surrender” to AI answers—accuracy and confidence track AI correctness rather than their own reasoning—and this pattern is large, robust across time pressure and incentives, and stronger for those who trust AI more and engage in less reflective thinking.**
